Brief Introduction:¶

I have selected to work with a dataset from Internet Movie Database (IMDb). IMDb provides crowdsourced and curated data about all aspects of the motion picture industry, at www.imdb.com. IMDb currently contains data on over 3.3 million movies and TV programs. In this dataset it has some preselected rows that will be used in this analysis below (ie. movie popularity, runtime, budget, revenue, director, genres, release date, votes...etc). The dataset contains alittle over 10,000 movies and their associated data.

Questions to Answer about the Dataset:¶

We will analyze the IMDb dataset and try to answer some of the questions below. These questions specifically are:

  • #1) Does budget have any corralation to overall ratings?
  • #2) How well does movie gross correlate with viewer ratings or awards?
  • #3) Does runtime affect the rating of a movie (ie. on average do longer movies have better ratings?) ?
  • #4) What months of the movie release dates had movies with the largest budget to produce?

    Conclusions¶

    Note: you can find most of the conclusions that was receommended to put in this section are put at the end of each of the questions where the analysis and obversations are better layed out.

    Repeated questions here for convenience:

    1) Does budget have any corralation to overall ratings?¶

    2) How well does movie gross correlate with viewer ratings or awards?</a></li>¶

    3) Does runtime affect the rating of a movie (ie. on average do longer movies have better ratings?) ?</a></li>¶

    4) What months of the movie release dates had movies with the largest budget to produce?</a></li>¶

    For this small analysis we made some edits and assumptions that may or may not have affected out conclusions. Some of these were:

    • We filtered out 7000 zeroed budgeted entries to 'normalize' the data
    • We didn't count budgets of less then 10,000 dollars which could contain some interesting low budget and high rated movies.
    • We don't want to raise the filter to high (ie. $1,000,000) as this might circumvent the question what we are trying to answer in question 1 (in a sense, do you have to spend alot of money to make a good movie that recieves good ratings)

    For question 1, as mentioned in the analysis, it didn't seem we could see a strong correlation in movie production budget spent that effected the rating it may receive. There are several factors that can explain why this is the case. For one, the dataset was only ~10,800 movies may have not taken into account some of the much more acclaimed movies. Remember from the intro, the IMDb holds data for over 3 million movies and shows. The dataset we were given to analyze had a mix of general box-office movies, series documentaries, and short film data points. Each of these types of media formats may promote different type of feedback and votes. As an example, someone that goes to the theatre and enjoys a Marvel adventure for 2 hours may want to join the global communitity and engage in its promotion as the greatest seen movie but may not downgrade it as it not worth their time to report on IMDb but maybe more in other social media. While someone that is the type to watch long series documentaries and foreign films may not see reporting to IMDb as a tool at all to register a vote. We simply would need to look at more datasets and have a infrastructure (ie. Hadoop) to truly analyze such a large dataset to get the real valid insights on these same questions. To add to this we can see movies with the highest production budgets (ie. > 300-400 million dollars) have only can average just alittle better rating of the mean budgets spent (A).

    For question 2, as stated we have some correlation for low movie gross revenue at low ratings (4-6) but then tended to widen the spread of all ranges of revenue as we move above a vote rating of 6. We did make a limitation by limiting the revenue to above $1 million. However, including these movies would probably affected our analysis as at worst it would provide some high ratings at low revenues (ie. unknown and less watch movies got some great revenue) resulting in even more ambigious results. We have some correlation but not strong to make any true predictions. One big issue with this limited analysis is not taken into account that some movies that recieved huge revenue streams would most likely came from repeated box office ticket sales on a global scale and might engage in newer viewing movie technologies (ie. Ultra AVX, IMAX 3D, D-Box setting, VIP theatres..etc) which usually comes with a price premium. These revenues might be an explanation for the outliers in observed in the area indicated in the direction of the green arrows of the graph (towards area B).

    For question 3, as stated preivously, we did see some correlation in average runtime movies (75 - 200 mins range) that the ratings (votes) seem to rise. But when we looked at the number of data points taken that represented those bars (bar chart in GREEN in the second chart) we could see a high amount of of the data (94%) weighted highly in the 75-125 mins range. We stopped the analysis short here because simply put we would need to have more movies of various lengths for the other bars (bins) to have a truly meaningful comparison. It would only make sense to continue the analysis if we had more data or we answered some more grandular questions that sit within this high dataset weight class which was not required in this case.

    For question 4, we saw from the Total Budget of Movies seems to have two noticable peaks (one for summer months [May, June, July] and another from winter months [Nov and Dec]. This is probably explained by understanding production companies seeing the when is the best time to release a movie. Good times are with weather is good and families are on vacation. However, this works as well during the end of the year (when weather is not so good) but this is usually considered 'holiday' session and most want to enjoy time out and generally will be in a spending mode. All these contribute to increasing the movie revenue streams, especially if the movie was voted highly and affects some to watch one movie two or more times or inspiring others to go see a movie (via social media, word of mouth...etc).

    I think the main conclusion from all these questions that we can definitively come to is a dataset of ten thousand movie entries doesn't seem to be enough to draw consist and obvious predictions.
